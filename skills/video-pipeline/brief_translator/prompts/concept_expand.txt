--- SENTENCE TEXT RULES (MOST IMPORTANT) ---

- Each concept's sentence_text must be an EXACT, UNMODIFIED substring of the
  narration text above. Copy-paste it character for character.
- Concepts must be in order: concept 1's text comes first in the narration,
  concept 2's text comes next, etc.
- Every single word of the narration must appear in exactly ONE concept's
  sentence_text. No gaps. No overlaps.
- Split at natural sentence or clause boundaries (periods, commas, semicolons).
- Do NOT correct typos, grammar, or punctuation in the narration. Copy it as-is.
- MAXIMUM 25 WORDS per concept's sentence_text. If a sentence is longer than
  25 words, split it at a natural clause boundary (comma, dash, semicolon).
  Never put more than 25 words into a single concept.
- MINIMUM 10 WORDS per concept's sentence_text. If a sentence is under 10
  words, merge it with its neighbor into one concept. A concept under 10
  words will display for less than 4 seconds — too fast for viewers to
  register the image.
- Aim for 12-20 words per concept (~5-8 seconds display time per image).
  Faster pacing keeps viewers engaged. Err on the side of shorter concepts.

--- VISUAL DESCRIPTION RULES ---

THE DOCUMENTARY CAMERA TEST:
Before writing any visual description, ask: "Could a real documentary camera
crew physically go to a location and film this?"
If YES → good. If NO → rewrite it.

NEVER use these in visual descriptions:
- Literal metaphor visualization (rivers of money, puppet strings, chess pieces)
- Abstract machinery (gears of government, engines of growth)
- Surreal/sci-fi elements (holographic, glowing objects, floating things)
- Lighting/camera/style language (Rembrandt lighting, shallow depth of field)
- Mood adjectives (moody, ominous, foreboding)

SCENE DESCRIPTIONS must contain:
- WHAT is in the scene (people, objects, environment — or NONE of these if the
  scene is a landscape, building exterior, data display, chart, or empty space)
- WHERE it takes place (specific, named or implied location)
- WHAT is happening (action, gesture, or state)
- SENSORY DETAILS that make the image feel real: specific materials (brushed
  steel, cracked leather, rain-slicked asphalt), textures (peeling paint,
  condensation on glass, ink-stained paper), spatial relationships (foreground/
  midground/background layers), scale cues (a figure dwarfed by architecture,
  a phone screen glowing in a dark room), and environmental atmosphere
  (steam rising from grates, dust motes in a shaft of light, fog rolling
  across a harbor)

NOT EVERY SCENE NEEDS A PERSON. Many of the strongest images are:
- An empty environment that tells a story (abandoned trading floor, locked
  vault door, fog-shrouded port with idle tankers)
- A data display or chart on a screen in context (monitor bank in a dim
  operations center showing price curves)
- An object in isolation (a classified folder on a desk, a cracked pipeline
  valve leaking into soil, stacked shipping containers)
- An aerial or satellite perspective (port from above, city grid at night,
  convoy on a desert highway)

Write 30-75 words per description. Mine the narration for concrete specifics —
numbers, place names, objects mentioned — and translate them into visible,
tangible details. The narration is your raw material; extract every visual cue
it contains rather than summarizing generically.

MATCH DESCRIPTION TO COMPOSITION:
- "wide": Full environment, layered depth — foreground detail, midground
  subject, background context. Figure small in frame if present.
- "medium": Subject from waist up, surrounded by environment details that
  establish context. Hands, desk objects, wall details all visible.
- "closeup": Tight on a specific detail — hands gripping a document, a screen
  displaying data, condensation on a glass, ink on a signature line. No wide
  context.
- "environmental": NO human figure. Architecture, objects, weather, or
  landscape telling the story alone. The emptiness or fullness of the space
  IS the subject.
- "portrait": Subject from chest up, expression and posture conveying intent.
  Background softened but identifiable.
- "overhead": Bird's-eye or satellite view looking straight down. Maps,
  layouts, formations, or patterns visible from above.
- "low_angle": Looking up at something imposing — a building facade, a figure
  on a balcony, the underside of a bridge, a ship's hull from waterline.

--- DIVERSITY RULES ---

- No two consecutive concepts may describe the same subject or setting.
- Vary compositions — don't put 3+ of the same composition in a row.
- Use different scales: exterior → interior → desk detail → document closeup.
- Use different actors: the CEO → the worker → the regulator → the consumer.
