CRITICAL RULES — READ ALL BEFORE STARTING:

--- SENTENCE TEXT RULES (MOST IMPORTANT) ---

- Each concept's sentence_text must be an EXACT, UNMODIFIED substring of the
  narration text above. Copy-paste it character for character.
- Concepts must be in order: concept 1's text comes first in the narration,
  concept 2's text comes next, etc.
- Every single word of the narration must appear in exactly ONE concept's
  sentence_text. No gaps. No overlaps.
- Split at natural sentence or clause boundaries (periods, commas, semicolons).
- Do NOT correct typos, grammar, or punctuation in the narration. Copy it as-is.
- MAXIMUM 35 WORDS per concept's sentence_text. If a sentence is longer than
  35 words, split it at a natural clause boundary (comma, dash, semicolon).
  Never put more than 35 words into a single concept.
- MINIMUM 12 WORDS per concept's sentence_text. If a sentence is under 12
  words, merge it with its neighbor into one concept. A concept under 12
  words will display for less than 5 seconds — too fast for viewers to
  register the image.
- Aim for 15-28 words per concept (~6-11 seconds display time per image).

--- VISUAL DESCRIPTION RULES ---

THE DOCUMENTARY CAMERA TEST:
Before writing any visual description, ask: "Could a real documentary camera
crew physically go to a location and film this?"
If YES → good. If NO → rewrite it.

NEVER use these in visual descriptions:
- Literal metaphor visualization (rivers of money, puppet strings, chess pieces)
- Abstract machinery (gears of government, engines of growth)
- Surreal/sci-fi elements (holographic, glowing objects, floating things)
- Lighting/camera/style language (Rembrandt lighting, shallow depth of field)
- Mood adjectives (moody, ominous, foreboding)

SUBSTITUTION TABLE — show the concrete reality:

| Systemic risk           | Chart on screen, emergency meeting, empty trading floor   |
| Secrecy/corruption      | Locked door, shredder, burner phone, unusual meeting      |

SCENE DESCRIPTIONS must ONLY contain:
- WHAT is in the scene (people, objects, environment)
- WHERE it takes place (specific location)
- WHAT is happening (action)
- KEY VISUAL DETAILS (objects, textures, materials, weather)

Keep descriptions 20-35 words. Be specific but concise.

CRITICAL — VISUAL DESCRIPTION QUALITY CHECK:
Your visual_description must NEVER contain words from the narration text.
It describes what the CAMERA SEES, not what the narrator SAYS.

BAD (restating narration):
  "This scenario requires several conditions that unlikely materialize"
  "Must convince commanders control independent military forces revenue"
  "Game theory suggests cooperation unstable without enforcement"

GOOD (what the camera sees):
  "Three Mexican military commanders hunched over maps in a concrete bunker, single bare bulb overhead, cold teal shadows"
  "Stacks of peso bills on a metal table beside an assault rifle, cartel insignia scratched into the wall"
  "Empty highway stretching through Jalisco desert, burned-out truck blocking both lanes, smoke rising"

If your visual_description reads like a summary of the narration, DELETE IT
and write what a documentary camera crew would actually film instead.

MATCH DESCRIPTION TO COMPOSITION:
- "wide": Full environment, figure small in frame
- "medium": Person from waist up in context
- "closeup": Specific detail, hands, objects, documents
- "environmental": No human figure, architecture/objects only
- "portrait": Person from chest up, face partially in shadow
- "overhead": Bird's-eye view, looking down
- "low_angle": Looking up at something imposing

--- DIVERSITY RULES ---

- No two consecutive concepts may describe the same subject or setting.
- Vary compositions — don't put 3+ of the same composition in a row.
- Use different scales: exterior → interior → desk detail → document closeup.
- Use different actors: the CEO → the worker → the regulator → the consumer.
